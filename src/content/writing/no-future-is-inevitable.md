# No Future Is Inevitable

I am old enough to start sentences with "I'm old enough to remember when…" and reminisce with political optimism.

I remember in 2016 thinking I'm going to get to have humane national maternity leave policies by the time I have kids. Or in 2020 believing our country was finally reckoning with our violent history of race and oppression. Or in 2021 thinking that the world-altering COVID-19 pandemic gave us all the evidence we needed that we could in fact make big, rapid, and coordinated changes to our society and economy to protect people. Workers finally had power and the government could in fact provide material support to suffering families.

But it's 2026 and I just hacked together a too-short maternity leave to welcome my daughter into the world and I'm thinking every day about how I'm going to explain to her that yes: we knew that fossil fuels were killing us and that capitalism wasn't working for most people and that democracy was being held hostage by fascists.

I am not alone in looking at the past decade and feeling — and the facts bear this out — that things got worse. The optimism I felt a decade ago was not the signal of an inevitably better future.

The arc of justice does not always bend forward.

To be frank: this is a shitty feeling. It is a feeling that can often be named despair.

But because I am, at my core, an optimist — and because as a parent I look into the wild and hungry eyes of a small human every day and have the humbling and glorious responsibility to show her all the amazing things of this world — I don't have the option of despair.

So I'm taking the hard lesson that the future isn't inevitable and turning it around to be a blessing.

---

Right now the future feels bleak. Every day we are told that AI is going to take everyone's jobs and that democracy is failing and that the record-breaking heatwaves we're experiencing now will be the norm. Many young people are on track to have a lower quality of life than their parents.

The litany of social ills is long and the muscles of imagining a more positive alternative are atrophied.

But I am drawn back to the mantra that once felt like a curse: *no future is inevitable.*

As a student of history, you have to hold two truths at the same time: the present is a product of all that came before, and the future is not deterministic.

Sure, the monopolistic tendencies of tech companies, the inability to regulate or prepare society for social media over the last 15 years, and the deep wealth inequality of the US over the past 50 years all culminate to make this a particularly worrisome moment for AI to upend our entire economic and social system.

But the dystopian world where only five companies rule the world, getting rich off the collective reflection of all human knowledge on the internet and replacing humans with AI workflows and chatbots — that isn't inevitable.

I repeat. This isn't inevitable. It will take coordinated, sustained effort to avert. And it will take imagination and an appetite for maneuvering in ambiguity and taking chances.

---

Again I look to history for inspiration, and I'm reminded of the 1980s. The Cold War is raging and two nuclear powers are locked in a struggle for political, military, and cultural dominance. Chernobyl has shown us the disastrous consequences of nuclear missteps.

Nuclear war feels inevitable.

And yet.

The Cold War ended not with bombs but in a kind of controlled avalanche. The conditions had been accumulating for years — economic decay, a ruinous war, cracks in the propaganda architecture — but nobody predicted the timing or the shape of it. When the moment came, it moved fast. The people who were ready — dissidents, reformers, organizers who had been doing unglamorous work for years without knowing if it would ever matter — suddenly found themselves inside a hinge moment.

That's the uncomfortable truth about how change actually happens. You can't manufacture the convergence. You can't schedule the unraveling. But you can be in position when it comes. You can be the person who knows what to do when the window opens.

---

There is a future where generations of Americans are fed up with their wellbeing being sacrificed for shareholder profits and a few enterprising senators successfully pass legislation for more public ownership of the AI companies built on public data.

There's a future that wakes up to the absurdity of single-use plastic and opts to adopt alternatives instead of poisoning our oceans and bodies with petroleum waste.

There's a future where the moral stain of childhood poverty in the wealthiest country in the world becomes too disgraceful to ignore.

There's a future where we apply the same licensing and insurance standards we have for cars to the other deadliest machine you can buy in the US: guns.

I'm old enough to remember that big changes can happen. I remember marriage equality getting passed, electing the first Black president, and the decriminalization and legalization of marijuana. But I'm young enough to have never seen my generation in a strong leadership position in government, or to remember a time when it really felt like government was effective and working for the people.

This is the future I want to make inevitable. Not because history promises it. Not because optimism is a personality trait I can't shake. But because the muscle of imagining a better world only grows if you use it — and I intend to use it every day, in every stubborn and creative way I can, until my daughter is old enough to take over and imagine further than I can.
