**That's it.** If I only had 15 words to broadcast one of my most deeply held beliefs about AI, this is what I'd put on a billboard. 

But luckily, we have longer form media at our disposal. Dear reader, I believe long form reading can save us from the worst consequences of AI: endless content, ravaged attention spans, and the devaluation of human skill and human beings. 

**So please, read on like it's 2008 and your attention hasn't been pinged to death.** 

There's a promise at the center of the AI moment: *infinite access to information.* 

Now of course not all information is created equally and not all content is knowledge but for the sake of simplicity I'm using information loosely here — the way the tech industry uses it. Media. Content. The stuff we read and engage with. And wow. What a promise. The entire sum of human knowledge, distillable, searchable, summarizable, available to anyone with a browser. It would be churlish not to acknowledge how extraordinary that is. 

But we've created this machine that can generate endless information without considering adequately the other side of the equation: how do we absorb it? How does this firehose of information change us? What gets lost in the artifice of intelligence? 

Our collective ability to receive information — to sit with it, wrestle with it, metabolize it into actual understanding — has been quietly degrading for years. And into that degraded soil, we are now planting the most powerful information tool ever built.

The teachers knew. They've been ringing the alarm for over a decade. Kids who can't read at grade level. Comprehension in freefall. Attention spans that can't hold a paragraph. Short fuses in classrooms designed for sustained thought. This isn't simply a case of "kids these days" so much as the canary in the coal mine warning us of an environment that has been systematically dismantling the conditions for deep attention.

The data backs them up. Year over year, reading rates decline. Time spent with long-form text shrinks. Maryanne Wolf's Reader, Come Home — one of the most important books I read last year — traces what actually happens to the reading brain when we stop using it. The neural pathways built through sustained reading — pathways for empathy, critical analysis, inference, reflection — don't just sit idle. They weaken. The brain is plastic in both directions.

**Enter the humble book.** A time tested technology for building human attention and transforming content (words) into knowledge. 

Here's the inference I think is sound even if correlation isn't causation: atrophying attention spans and rising susceptibility to misinformation are not unrelated to declining reading habits. When we stop practicing the slow work of following a complex argument across two hundred pages, we lose the muscle for it. And a person without that muscle is far more vulnerable to the compressed, decontextualized, emotionally charged information that dominates our feeds.

This was the condition before AI. Now we've introduced a technology that, among its many gifts, offers to read for us. I want to be careful here, because I'm not making a simple anti-AI argument. I use these tools. I build with them and I teach with them. I believe they can be genuinely useful.

But I keep thinking about the rat park experiments. In the famous studies, isolated rats with access to heroin became addicted. It sounds like straightforward case of drugs = bad.

But when researchers gave rats a rich environment — space, social connection, things to do and explore — they largely ignored the heroin, even when it was available. The drug wasn't the whole story. The environment was the story.
AI isn't inherently dangerous. There's a version of a more connected, secure, peaceful world that adopts AI for human flourishing. I believe there's a way to use and adopt AI that could put us in touch with our most human capabilities: deep learning, creativity, and connection. 

But that's not our world, not even close. AI is landing in a particular environment — one already characterized by loneliness, shortened attention, weakened critical faculties, and a hunger for shortcuts. In that environment, the features that make AI most seductive are the same ones most likely to accelerate the damage.

Summarize this article. Explain this concept like I'm five. Give me the takeaways. Skip to the answer. Our rat brains don't have the buffer to resist the drugs of a good shortcut. 

The most human truth is that there are no shortcuts to learning. There are only shortcuts to the feeling of learning. And those are very different things.

Deep reading takes time. That's one of the key features. We only call it a bug when we're impatient or strapped for time (see: modern life). Sitting down with a book is an invitation to the sustained, effortful, non-linear practice in receiving and processing complex experience. Maryanne Wolf writes that reading is one of the few activities that exercises the full range of human cognitive capacity simultaneously. It builds the circuitry for everything we most need: critical thinking, empathy, the tolerance for ambiguity, the ability to hold an idea long enough to actually think about it.

All this from a BOOK! An invention that predates the first century! 

So here is my hot take, offered to AI evangelists and AI skeptics with equal conviction:

**The most radical thing you can do in the age of AI is read a book.**

Not as protest or nostalgia but as the practice we all need to guard against becoming a cage full of addicted rats. 

Reading rebuilds the attentional capacity that makes you a sovereign user of any tool, including this one. It trains the critical faculties that let you evaluate AI output rather than absorb it. It gives you the slow experience of muddling through a hard idea and coming out the other side changed — which is the only experience that actually teaches you how ideas work.

This is what deep reading offers us and the skills so desperately needed in an AI age. 

So regardless of if you're feeling excitement or existential dread about AI (I feel both everyday); put down the phone, pick up something long and difficult, and practice being a human who thinks.
